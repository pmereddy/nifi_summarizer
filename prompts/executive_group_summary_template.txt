Instructions/Features to include:
Business-Oriented Source Description: Start by describing where the data comes from in business terms. Identify the source system or origin of the data in a way that’s meaningful to the stakeholder. For example: “Data originates from the Customer Orders web portal" or “The process begins by pulling data from the Finance department’s FTP server each morning." Avoid mentioning NiFi processor names like “GetFile" or “ListenHTTP" – instead, say “receives files" or “listens for incoming HTTP submissions" in a general sense. The idea is to frame the source as a business system or a real-world data source, not a NiFi component.
Simplified Transformation Description: Summarize what happens to the data as it moves through the flow in broad strokes. Focus on the purpose of each major step, not the mechanism. For instance, instead of “ExecuteScript runs a Groovy script to parse records," say “the data is validated and parsed to ensure it’s correctly formatted." Instead of “RouteOnAttribute splits the flow," say “the process separates VIP customers from standard customers to tailor the handling of each group." Each major transformation or decision should be described in terms of its business logic or benefit: e.g., “duplicate entries are removed," “invalid records are filtered out," “records are aggregated by region for reporting efficiency," etc. Keep these descriptions concise and free of technical configuration details – an executive doesn’t need to know how often something runs or the exact schema, only that “the data is checked for errors and enriched with relevant info," for example.
Highlight Business Rules and Decisions: If the flow implements any business rules or conditional logic, describe those rules at a high level. For example, “Orders above a certain value are flagged and routed for priority handling," or “If a customer is marked as VIP, their data is routed on a faster track for immediate processing." This tells the stakeholder how business policies are enforced by the system. Do not refer to these as “RouteOnAttribute" or “decision node" – just explain the rule itself (“high-value orders trigger a special alert"). Emphasize how these rules benefit the business (e.g., “ensuring that important clients get faster service" or “preventing invalid data from entering the billing system").
Outcome and Destination: Clearly state where the data ends up and the business outcome or purpose of that destination. For example: “Finally, the refined data is loaded into the central Data Warehouse, where it becomes available for analytics and reporting," or “The process ends by sending an alert email to the support team if errors are detected, and storing clean data in the Sales database for daily operations." If multiple outputs exist, frame each in terms of what value it provides (“critical notifications are emailed to administrators, while all valid transactions are stored in the ERP system"). Avoid internal names like “Output Port" or “PutEmail" – instead say “an email is sent," “data is saved to X system," etc.
Abstract Technical Details: Omit or translate any NiFi-specific or low-level technical details such as scheduling, thread counts, queue sizes, or processor names. These are usually irrelevant to a business audience and can cause confusion. For instance, rather than saying “the flow uses a Cron-driven scheduling at 5-minute intervals," simply say “data is collected and updated every few minutes" (if timing is even necessary to mention). Only include an operational detail if it has a clear business impact or risk. One example might be mentioning reliability: “The system is designed to automatically retry and route failed records to an error queue, ensuring that issues in one part don’t halt the entire process." This conveys robustness without technical jargon.
Focus on Efficiency and Reliability Benefits: It’s often useful to reassure the executive about the system’s efficiency, reliability, and alignment with business goals. Mention features like “parallel processing" or “automated error handling" in a non-technical way if they are important. For example: “The data processing is streamlined and automated – it handles thousands of records in parallel, so new data is available within minutes," or “Built-in checks and balances ensure bad data is caught and isolated, preventing downstream errors in financial reports." These points help the stakeholder understand the value and robustness of the system.
Keep it High-Level and Concise: The overall summary should be relatively short and free of NiFi terminology. It can be a few short paragraphs or a set of bulleted highlights. Each paragraph/bullet should be 1-3 sentences, focusing on one aspect: Source, Processing/Transformation, Destination/Outcome, and possibly Reliability/Performance if worth noting. The tone should be business-friendly – for instance, talk about “information" instead of “FlowFiles," “system" or “workflow" instead of “NiFi flow," and “business rules" instead of “processor logic." Remember, the executive cares about what the system accomplishes (improving data quality, speeding up delivery, connecting systems) more than how it technically works.
Example (for context): “This data pipeline collects customer order data from our e-commerce platform and prepares it for analysis. It automatically checks each order for completeness and accuracy, filtering out any duplicates or errors. Valid orders are then categorized by customer type – for instance, VIP customer orders are flagged for special handling. Once processed, all the clean, categorized order information is sent to the company’s Data Warehouse and also queued for real-time dashboard updates. The design ensures that important orders are prioritized and that any bad data is caught early, helping our operations run smoothly with reliable data input."
(In this example, notice the absence of technical terms: it doesn’t mention NiFi, processors, or scheduling, but an executive would grasp what the pipeline does and why it’s beneficial.)
